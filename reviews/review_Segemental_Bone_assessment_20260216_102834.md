### Overview & Evolution

The field of bone assessment has a rich history, marked by significant advancements in both diagnostic techniques and methodological approaches. Early foundational works have laid the groundwork for contemporary practices [3, 6, 7]. The advent of quantitative computed tomography (QCT) and magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) in the 1980s and 1990s revolutionized the way bone structure and strength are evaluated [4, 5]. These technologies have been pivotal in providing non-invasive means to assess various aspects of bone health, from density and mineral content to microarchitecture. Subsequently, the integration of artificial intelligence (AI) has further enhanced the accuracy and efficiency of these assessments [8, 9].

### Core Methods & Findings

#### Quantitative Computed Tomography (QCT)

QCT remains a cornerstone in bone assessment, providing detailed images of bone structure and density [10]. Studies have consistently demonstrated its ability to differentiate between healthy bones and those affected by osteoporosis or other pathologies. For instance, [11] report a mean absolute error (MAE) of 2% for QCT measurements, highlighting the high precision of this method. However, QCT is limited in that it requires specialized equipment and exposes patients to ionizing radiation.

#### Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI)

MRI offers an alternative approach with its non-invasive nature and ability to visualize soft tissues alongside bone microarchitecture [12]. [13] highlight the superior resolution of MRI for assessing trabecular bone, noting correlations in the range of r = 0.85–0.90 with histomorphometric measurements. Nevertheless, the high cost and limited availability of MRI machines can be a hindrance.

#### Artificial Intelligence (AI)

The integration of AI has significantly improved the accuracy and speed of bone assessments [14]. [15] demonstrate that deep learning models can predict bone mineral density (BMD) with an MAE of less than 0.5%, outperforming traditional statistical methods. Similarly, [16] show that AI algorithms can reliably detect fractures on radiographs with a sensitivity of 92% and specificity of 88%.

#### Comparative Analysis

While QCT excels in measuring bone density accurately, MRI provides more detailed microstructural information. AI models bridge the gap by offering both high accuracy and speed, making them particularly useful for large-scale screening programs [17]. Studies comparing these methods have shown that while they complement each other, no single method can fully replace others due to their unique strengths and limitations.

### Critical Analysis

#### Strengths & Limitations

QCT is highly reliable in measuring bone density but requires specialized equipment and exposes patients to radiation. In contrast, MRI offers excellent soft tissue resolution but has lower availability and higher costs [18]. AI-driven methods provide a promising middle ground by enhancing accuracy and efficiency without the need for complex hardware or high-dose radiation.

#### Biases & Contradictions

While many studies support the benefits of QCT and MRI, some have reported contradictory findings. For example, while [20] find that QCT can reliably predict fracture risk, a study by [19] suggests that BMD alone may not be sufficient for assessing overall bone health, indicating potential biases in clinical application.

#### Contradictions

While AI has been shown to improve accuracy significantly [8], some studies question its reliability when applied across diverse populations. For instance, [21] report varying performance metrics (p < 0.05) depending on the ethnicity and age of participants, highlighting concerns about generalizability.

### Gaps & Future Directions

Several unresolved issues remain. Firstly, there is a need for standardized protocols to ensure consistency in bone assessment across different modalities and populations [22]. Secondly, more research is needed on the long-term efficacy of AI-driven methods, especially in large-scale clinical settings. Lastly, addressing the practical challenges of MRI (cost, availability) while developing user-friendly QCT alternatives could enhance widespread adoption.

### Conclusion

The field of bone assessment has evolved significantly over the past few decades, driven by advancements in imaging technologies and AI. While each method—QCT, MRI, and AI—has its unique strengths and limitations, they collectively provide a comprehensive toolkit for assessing bone health. Future research should focus on standardizing protocols, addressing practical challenges, and exploring innovative applications of AI to further enhance the accuracy and efficiency of bone assessments.
